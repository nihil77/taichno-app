History of Taijiquan
+

Taijiquan, also known as Tai Chi, is an ancient Chinese martial art with a rich history that dates back centuries. It originated in the Wudang Mountains of China and was developed by Taoist monk Zhang Sanfeng.

Originally conceived as a martial art for self-defense, Taijiquan evolved over time to incorporate principles of Taoism, Chinese medicine, and philosophy. It emphasizes the cultivation of internal energy (qi) and harmony between mind, body, and spirit.

Today, Taijiquan is practiced worldwide for its health benefits, stress reduction, and spiritual growth. It has various styles, including Chen, Yang, Wu, and Sun styles, each with its own unique characteristics and forms.

Stances of Taijiquan
+

Horse Stance (Ma Bu): Provides stability and strength, resembling the stance of a horse.

Bow-Arrow Stance (Gong JianBu): Emulates the posture of shooting a bow and arrow, enhancing balance and focus.

Sitting on Crossed Legs Stance (Zuo Pan Bu): Improves flexibility and relaxation, commonly used in meditation.

Four-Six Stance (Si Liu Bu): Enhances leg strength and stability, resembling the stance of a martial artist.

Tame the Tiger Stance (Fu Hu Bu): Fosters agility and agility, mimicking the readiness to face a tiger.

False Stance (Xuan Ji Bu or Xu Bu): Challenges balance and coordination, useful for advanced practitioners.

Golden Rooster Standing on One Leg Stance (Jin Gi Du Li): Develops concentration and balance, symbolizing the stance of a rooster.

Squat Stance (Zuo Dun): Builds leg strength and endurance, resembling a deep squat position.
